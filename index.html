01 — What Moved Markets Today

The Big Story: Inflation Cools — What It Actually Means

Last week the Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that the Consumer Price Index rose 2.7% year-over-year in June, down from 3.0% in May. That sounds like good news — and in many ways it is — but the story underneath the headline number is more nuanced.

What cooled down

Energy prices fell sharply, pulling the overall number lower. Gasoline is cheaper than it was a year ago, and utility costs dropped. These are real savings for households.

What stayed sticky

Services inflation — think rent, insurance, and dining out — remains elevated near 4%. This is the part the Fed watches most closely because it reflects underlying demand in the economy.

The Fed's target is 2%. We're closer than we've been, but services stickiness means the central bank is unlikely to cut rates aggressively. Markets currently price in one or two cuts before year-end, likely starting in September. Lower rates matter for your investments: they tend to lift bond prices, reduce borrowing costs, and often support stock valuations — especially in growth sectors.

03 — The Chain Reaction

Earnings Season: The Bank Reports You Need to Follow

This week marks the heart of early earnings season with three major bank reports. Here's what to watch and why it matters beyond just stock prices.

Goldman Sachs — Tuesday

Watch investment banking revenue. A rebound here signals that deal-making and IPO activity is returning. Goldman also gives clues about M&A appetite among large corporations — a leading indicator of corporate confidence.

Bank of America — Tuesday

BofA is the most rate-sensitive of the big banks. Their net interest income (the profit from lending) will reveal how higher-for-longer rates are helping — or starting to hurt — their core business as borrowers strain.

Morgan Stanley — Wednesday

Wealth management revenue is the key metric. Strong results here reflect healthy consumer investment activity. Their asset management division also mirrors broader market sentiment among affluent investors.

Beyond individual stocks, bank earnings collectively paint a picture of the economy's health: consumer credit quality, corporate borrowing appetite, and financial system stress or stability.

04 — What It Means

Career & Money: Navigating a Shifting Job Market

The June jobs report showed the labor market is cooling but not cracking. Unemployment ticked up to 4.1% — still historically low — while wage growth slowed to 3.9% annually. Here's what this means for you.

If you're job hunting

The market is more competitive than 2021–2023. Employers are taking longer to hire and negotiating harder on salary. Research market rates carefully using tools like Levels.fyi, LinkedIn Salary, and Glassdoor before any offer conversation.

If you're employed

Wage growth is slowing, meaning raises may be smaller this year. Now is the time to document your impact and have a compensation conversation proactively — don't wait for annual reviews.

Sectors still hiring strongly

Healthcare, defense technology, infrastructure, and AI-adjacent roles continue to add jobs. Financial services hiring is mixed — wealth management up, investment banking still lean.

Side income reality check

Freelance and gig income is facing more competition. Platforms are saturated in writing and design. High-value skills like data analysis, financial modeling, and technical consulting still command premium rates.

05 — Market Sector Lens

Personal Finance Move of the Week: Max Your HYSA Before Rates Drop

High-yield savings accounts are still paying 4.5–5.2% APY at online banks — but this window may be closing. If the Fed cuts rates in September as expected, those yields will fall within weeks.

Step 1: Calculate your emergency fund target — typically 3–6 months of essential expenses.
Step 2: Check your current savings rate. If it's below 4.5%, you're leaving real money on the table.
Step 3: Compare top HYSAs: Marcus by Goldman Sachs, SoFi, Ally, and Discover consistently rank highest.
Step 4: Consider laddering short-term CDs (3–6 month) to lock in rates if you have cash you won't need immediately.
Step 5: Once your emergency fund is set, redirect additional cash to your investment accounts — don't hoard excess cash at the expense of long-term growth.

A fully funded emergency fund in a high-yield account is the foundation everything else is built on. Get this right first.

06 — Finance Career Lens

Global Lens: China's Export Surge and What It Signals

China reported export growth of 8.6% year-over-year in June, well above expectations. This matters to your portfolio even if you don't own Chinese stocks directly.

Why it matters globally

Strong Chinese exports mean global supply chains are running efficiently and Chinese manufacturers are competitive on price. This is disinflationary — it helps keep goods prices low in the US and Europe, which actually supports the case for rate cuts.

The tension underneath

The surge is partly driven by front-loading before anticipated tariffs. The US and EU are both considering additional tariffs on Chinese EVs, steel, and solar panels. Trade friction could reverse these flows quickly.

For investors, this dynamic boosts companies that source from China (consumer goods, electronics retailers) while putting pressure on domestic manufacturers competing with cheaper imports. Watch industrial and materials stocks for volatility on any new tariff announcements.

07 — Why This Matters for Interviews This Week

Investing Concept: Understanding Forward P/E Ratios

You'll hear analysts say the market is 'expensive' or 'cheap' based on P/E ratios. Here's how to actually understand what that means.

What P/E means

Price-to-Earnings ratio = Stock price divided by earnings per share. It tells you how much investors are paying for each dollar of profit. A P/E of 21 means investors pay $21 for every $1 of annual earnings.

Forward vs. Trailing

Trailing P/E uses past earnings. Forward P/E uses analyst estimates of future earnings. Forward is more useful for investing decisions because markets are forward-looking — but it's also less reliable since estimates can be wrong.

How to use it

Compare a stock's forward P/E to its own history and to its sector peers. A stock with a forward P/E of 30 isn't automatically overvalued if it's growing earnings 25% annually. Context is everything.

The current market context

The S&P 500's forward P/E of ~21x is above its long-term average of 18x. This means the market is pricing in continued strong earnings growth. If earnings disappoint, valuations compress and prices fall — which is the bear case heading into the second half of 2025.

08 — Term of the Day
Net Interest Margin (NIM)

Net Interest Margin is the difference between the interest income a bank earns on loans and investments versus the interest it pays out on deposits, expressed as a percentage of its interest-earning assets. A higher NIM means the bank is more profitably deploying the money it holds. During the rate-hiking cycle, most banks saw NIMs improve significantly. Now, as rates may fall and deposit competition intensifies, NIM compression is a key risk watch for bank earnings this week.
