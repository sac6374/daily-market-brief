Smart in 30 Seconds

Markets are being pulled in three directions at once: Mag 7 earnings mostly beat but AI capex hikes are rattling investors worried about ROI timelines; oil surged to a 4-year high of $126 on Iran blockade escalation fears before retreating; and the Fed held rates steady but three voting members dissented — the most internal discord since 1992 — pushing the 10-year yield above 4.4% and signaling that cuts are off the table for now. S&P 500 is on track for its best monthly gain in over five years despite the chaos.

01 — What Moved Markets Today

Mag 7 Beats, Meta Bleeds, Oil Spikes, and the Fed Splits

Alphabet Rockets — Google Cloud Up 63% YoY, Best of the Bunch

Alphabet delivered the cleanest print of the Mag 7 cluster. Q1 revenue hit $109.9 billion, up from analyst estimates of $107.2 billion — a $2.7 billion beat. Google Cloud revenue surged 63% year-over-year to $20.02 billion, smashing the $18.05 billion consensus. Shares climbed as much as 6% in after-hours trading. This matters because cloud growth is the market's real-time scorecard for AI ROI — and Alphabet just posted the strongest number in its history.

Meta Drops 9% — Record Revenue Can't Offset $145B Capex Shock

Meta posted Q1 revenue of $56.3 billion, up 33.1% year-over-year, beating estimates by $760 million. EPS of $10.44 crushed expectations by $3.78. But none of that mattered when management raised full-year capex guidance to $125–$145 billion, up from a prior range of $115–$135 billion. Shares fell 9% — a reminder that the market right now is less interested in how much you earned and more focused on how much you're burning.

Brent Briefly Hits $126 — Iran Blockade Threatens Further Military Escalation

Brent crude spiked to $126.41 per barrel intraday — a wartime high and its highest level since 2022 — before retreating to around $114. The catalyst: Axios reported that U.S. Central Command is briefing President Trump on expanded military options against Iran. Trump has already rejected Iran's proposal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, and the U.S. naval blockade remains in place until a nuclear deal is reached. The IEA has called the supply disruption the "largest in history," with daily tanker transits through the Strait plunging to single digits.

Fed Holds, But Three Dissenters Send a Hawkish Signal Markets Couldn't Ignore

The Fed kept the benchmark federal funds rate unchanged at 3.50%–3.75%, as expected. The shock came from the vote: three members dissented against language suggesting the Fed could eventually resume rate cuts — the most internal discord since 1992. Markets read this as a hawkish pivot. The 10-year yield jumped 7 basis points to 4.42% on Wednesday, and traders are now pricing in roughly a one-in-three chance of a rate hike by April 2027. Chair Powell also confirmed he will remain on the Board of Governors past his chairmanship while the headquarters renovation probe continues.

02 — Why It Mattered

Why investors cared.

All three of the market's dominant forces — earnings, energy, and monetary policy — printed on the same day, and none gave a clean signal. Alphabet proved AI spending can translate to revenue. Meta proved it can also terrify investors. Oil proved the Iran war premium is not going away. And the Fed proved that "hold" no longer means "neutral" when three governors are itching to hike.

The market desperately wants to believe AI capex will pay off, but every time a hyperscaler raises its spending guide, investors panic about the timeline to returns. Meanwhile, $114+ Brent oil is quietly functioning like a tax on every other sector — crimping consumer spending, pressuring airlines and industrials, and complicating the Fed's path. The S&P 500 heading for its best monthly gain in five years while oil is at war-level highs and the Fed just flashed hawkish signals is a tension that has to resolve itself — one way or another — in the next quarter.

04 — What It Means

Breaking it down.

Rates & Treasury Yields

The 10-year Treasury yield sits at 4.43% — up roughly 7 basis points from Tuesday — after the Fed's hold was paired with a three-member hawkish dissent, the most since 1992. The 2-year yield climbed above 3.94%, reflecting markets repricing the probability of a hike rather than a cut in the next 12 months. Higher yields matter because they raise the discount rate applied to future earnings — which is exactly why high-multiple tech stocks like Meta sold off even on a revenue beat.

Stocks & Indexes

The S&P 500 is essentially flat on the day at 7,139 — a remarkable non-event given the volume of news. Alphabet is the session's standout winner, up sharply on its cloud beat. Meta is the biggest drag after its capex hike. The Dow is outperforming both major indexes, up over 1%, because its composition skews toward industrials and healthcare which benefit from the energy sector's tailwind. April is still on track to be the S&P's best month in more than five years — up roughly 12% — driven by the Iran war-era energy rally and the AI earnings resurgence.

Broader Economy

Oil above $100 is functioning as a macro headwind for consumers and a windfall for energy producers — energy sector earnings are expected to rise roughly 40% year-over-year in Q1, driven by the Iran conflict. Average IRS tax refunds are up 11% this year to approximately $3,400, which is partially offsetting the drag from elevated gasoline and fuel costs. S&P 500 Q1 earnings overall are tracking toward +14.5% year-over-year growth — the sixth consecutive quarter of double-digit expansion — suggesting the real economy is absorbing geopolitical shocks better than expected.

05 — Market Sector Lens

Who's moving and why.

Energy — Surging — Best Sector of 2026

Energy is the unambiguous winner of the Iran war era. Brent above $114 and WTI above $105 have driven energy sector earnings estimates up roughly 40% year-over-year — the highest revision of any S&P 500 sector. U.S. crude exports have surged to record levels above 6 million barrels per day as buyers globally seek non-Hormuz supply. If the blockade holds through Q2, energy earnings could revise even higher.

Technology — Mixed — Cloud Wins, Capex Concerns Linger

Tech is not one story right now — it's two. Cloud infrastructure (Alphabet, Microsoft Azure) is delivering stunning revenue growth that validates AI spending. But the capex arms race is creating investor anxiety about how long until returns materialize. The IT sector is projected to post 46% earnings growth in Q1, leading all sectors — but Meta's 9% drop on a beat shows the market will punish anyone who raises spending without a clear ROI bridge.

Consumer Discretionary — Under Pressure — High Oil Is a Consumer Tax

Elevated energy prices are quietly compressing discretionary spending. Q2 earnings estimates for the consumer discretionary sector have been cut more aggressively than almost any other group, reflecting the drag from higher fuel costs. However, the 11% jump in average IRS tax refunds to $3,400 is providing a partial buffer — households are spending some of that windfall, delaying what could be a more painful squeeze later in the year.

Financials & Industrials — Diverging — Industrials Outperform, Financials Lag

Industrials are among the top performers in 2026, up over 10% YTD, driven by AI data center construction demand and energy infrastructure buildout — Caterpillar is up 41% this year alone. Financials, on the other hand, are down about 5% YTD as the higher-for-longer rate environment pressures net interest margins and loan growth. The sector also faces uncertainty around the Fed succession and potential regulatory shifts as Kevin Warsh moves toward confirmation.

06 — Finance Career Lens

What people in finance are actually thinking about today.

For everyone in finance

The core skill being tested right now is separating signal from noise in earnings reports. A "beat" means nothing if the guidance spooked the market — Meta proved that today. When you read an earnings release, train yourself to look past the headline EPS number and go straight to forward guidance, capex plans, and segment growth rates. That's where companies telegraph what they actually believe about the next 12 months.

Wealth Management

Oil at $114 and a hawkish Fed are forcing wealth managers to rethink portfolio duration and commodity allocation simultaneously. For wealth management professionals, this environment is a real-time stress test of client portfolio construction. High oil prices are boosting energy allocations but suppressing bond returns, while the Fed's hawkish dissent signals that the "rate cut" thesis that supported duration plays is weakening. WM teams are actively debating whether to trim long-duration fixed income exposure, add commodity-linked assets, or hold through what may be a transitory war premium. In client conversations, the question is whether the Iran conflict is a structural repricing or a spike — and the answer shapes everything from asset allocation to withdrawal sequencing for retirees.

Equity Research

Mag 7 earnings week is the Super Bowl for equity research analysts — and today shows exactly why the job is hard. Equity research analysts covering tech spent weeks building earnings models for Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet, and Amazon — all reporting on the same night. Today's session illustrates the core challenge: Meta beat on every financial metric and still dropped 9% because analysts had to interpret whether a $20 billion capex guide raise was bullish (AI conviction) or bearish (margin pressure). ER analysts who can frame that nuance clearly — in writing, in 24 hours — are the ones who build reputation. If you're interviewing for ER roles, be ready to walk through how you'd model capex sensitivity and what metrics you'd use to value a hyperscaler.

Energy Investment Banking

The Iran war has created one of the most active deal environments in energy banking in a decade. With WTI above $105 and U.S. crude exports at record highs, energy companies are flush with cash flow and actively exploring M&A, refinancing, and infrastructure deals. Energy IB teams are advising on midstream expansions, LNG terminal financing, and strategic acquisitions as buyers rush to lock in non-Hormuz supply chains. The IEA's forecast of the sharpest quarterly demand decline since COVID adds complexity — bankers have to model scenarios where the conflict resolves quickly versus drags into 2027. If you're targeting energy IB, understand the Strait of Hormuz supply mechanics cold — it will come up in every interview this cycle.

Macro / Global Markets Trading

Today is a masterclass in how three independent macro shocks become one interconnected trade. The Fed's hawkish dissent, Iran's oil shock, and Mag 7 capex escalation are not separate stories for macro traders — they're one position: long energy, short long-duration bonds, and selectively long tech names with cloud revenue visibility. Macro desks at banks and hedge funds are actively trading the Strait of Hormuz reopening probability as a binary event that would simultaneously crash oil, rally bonds, and reprice Fed expectations. If you want to work on a global macro desk, be able to articulate how a single geopolitical event — like the Hormuz closure — cascades through rates, equities, FX, and commodities all at once.

08 — Term of the Day
Hawkish Dissent

When Federal Reserve voting members formally oppose a policy decision or its accompanying statement because they believe the stance is insufficiently focused on fighting inflation — typically signaling they would prefer higher rates or fewer cuts than the majority.
