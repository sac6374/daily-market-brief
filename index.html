Smart in 30 Seconds

The Fed held rates steady for a third straight meeting in what was likely Powell's final press conference as chair — but a historic 8-4 dissent vote spooked bond markets, pushing the 10-year yield up 5 bps to ~4.40%. Meanwhile, four Magnificent Seven companies reported after Wednesday's bell: Alphabet and Amazon surged, Meta sold off on massive capex hikes, and Microsoft beat but warned on spending. Apple reports tonight, and oil remains above $100 with a U.S. naval blockade still choking the Strait of Hormuz.

01 — What Moved Markets Today

The Day Four Things Competed for the Headline

Fed's Most Divided Vote Since 1992 Sends Yields Higher

The FOMC voted 8-4 to hold the benchmark rate at 3.5%–3.75% — the most splintered result in 34 years. Three regional Fed presidents dissented hawkishly, pushing back against any dovish forward guidance, while one Trump appointee dissented the other way, wanting a cut. That split matters: it signals the Fed's next chair, Kevin Warsh (whose nomination cleared the Senate Banking Committee), may inherit a genuinely fractured committee. Yields jumped on the news because more hawks = lower odds of near-term rate cuts = bonds reprice lower, yields higher.

Alphabet Rockets, Meta Sinks: AI Capex Split the Mag 7

All four Mag 7 companies that reported beat earnings estimates — but the market reaction was anything but uniform. Alphabet surged after Google Cloud revenue jumped 63% year-over-year to $20 billion, blowing past the $18 billion estimate and validating AI infrastructure spend. Meta fell sharply after raising its full-year capex guidance to $125–$145 billion (up from $115–$135 billion), spooking investors who want AI monetization now, not more spending. Microsoft beat on EPS ($4.27 vs. $4.06 expected) with Azure up 40%, but capex projections of ~$190 billion for the full year kept the stock in check. The takeaway: the market rewards AI revenue proof, punishes AI spend promises.

Iran Blockade Pushes Oil Above $110 Before Demand Reality Bites

WTI briefly surged above $110 and Brent spiked above $114 after reports that Trump was being briefed on expanded military options against Iran. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, cutting off a major share of global oil flows. However, prices pulled back intraday as the IEA flagged demand destruction — projecting global oil demand to contract by 80,000 barrels per day this year, the sharpest decline since COVID. The tension between a genuine supply shock and collapsing demand is what's keeping oil volatile rather than just spiking endlessly.

Apple on Deck: Tim Cook's Final Earnings Call as CEO

Apple reports Q2 2026 earnings after today's bell, with analysts expecting EPS of ~$1.95 on revenue of ~$109.7 billion — roughly 15% above the year-ago quarter. The story isn't just the numbers: Tim Cook announced plans to hand the CEO role to John Ternus in September, so investors will be listening for leadership transition commentary, AI product strategy, and any color on memory cost pressures squeezing hardware margins. iPhone shipments in China reportedly rose ~20% in Q1 2026, suggesting demand holds, but supply chain costs from the AI infrastructure boom are a wild card.

02 — Why It Mattered

Why investors cared.

This week was arguably the most consequential earnings-and-policy window of 2026. Five Mag 7 companies reported, the Fed delivered a surprise dissent drama, Powell signaled he's staying on as governor (preserving some institutional independence), and oil oscillated above $100 on war headlines. The S&P 500 is on pace for its best monthly gain in over five years, up more than 12% in April, making today's flat close feel like calm after a very loud storm.

The market is simultaneously pricing in strong corporate earnings growth (S&P 500 Q1 EPS up ~14% YoY) and an energy shock that is well above the Fed's inflation target — and those two things can't coexist forever. If oil stays high and the hawkish Fed dissents win the narrative under Warsh, rate-cut hopes evaporate, and the expensive valuations on tech become harder to justify. Something has to give: either geopolitics cools and oil retreats, or earnings need to keep running hard enough to hold multiples.

04 — What It Means

Breaking it down.

Rates & Treasury Yields

The 10-year Treasury yield climbed ~5 bps to roughly 4.40% after the Fed's surprise four-dissent vote — up from 4.35% the prior day and meaningfully higher than the 4.10% level seen a month ago. Higher yields tighten financial conditions without the Fed doing anything formal, pressuring long-duration assets like growth stocks. The 2-year yield also jumped sharply on the dissent news, signaling that markets are repricing any near-term cut expectations lower.

Stocks & Indexes

The S&P 500 is essentially flat on the day at ~7,139, but that masks a wide dispersion underneath: Alphabet surged, Eli Lilly spiked ~7% on strong GLP-1 drug sales (Mounjaro up 125% YoY), Meta fell ~9%, and Qualcomm jumped ~11% on AI silicon demand signals. The Dow is modestly positive while the Nasdaq dips, reflecting that heavy AI-spenders are getting punished while companies showing AI payoff are rewarded. April closes as one of the strongest months for the S&P 500 in years, up more than 12% from the March 30 low.

Broader Economy

Thursday's PCE inflation data came in mostly in line with expectations — stubborn but not accelerating — while GDP growth came in slightly below forecasts, a combination that keeps stagflation risk alive as a narrative. The IEA's demand destruction warning on oil is a rare bit of macro relief: if high energy prices are already throttling consumption, the supply shock may self-correct before the Fed has to act. Tax refunds averaging $3,400 this year — up 11% from last year — are providing a partial household offset against elevated fuel costs.

05 — Market Sector Lens

Who's moving and why.

Technology — Mixed — rewarding proof, punishing promises

Alphabet and Microsoft beat and showed real AI revenue traction (Google Cloud +63% YoY, Azure +40%), while Meta's capex escalation to up to $145 billion triggered a selloff. The sector's internal divide is sharpening: investors are no longer giving blanket credit for AI spend — they want the revenue line to move.

Energy — Volatile — supply shock vs. demand destruction

Oil is up ~77% versus a year ago (Brent ~$110 vs ~$62 last year), making energy the top-performing S&P 500 sector in 2026 with estimated earnings up nearly 40% YoY. But the IEA's forecast of an 80,000 bpd demand contraction signals the high-price environment is starting to kill demand — a ceiling may be forming even with the Hormuz blockade in place.

Health Care — Bright spot — GLP-1 drugs carrying the sector

Eli Lilly spiked ~7% after Mounjaro (diabetes) sales rose 125% and Zepbound (obesity) rose 80% YoY, with the company raising its full-year guidance to $82–$85 billion in sales. Health care as a whole is down ~5% YTD due to macro headwinds, but GLP-1 names are the clear outlier and a focal point for investors in the space.

Financials — Under pressure — yields up, cut hopes fading

The hawkish Fed dissent and rising yields are a mixed bag for financials: higher rates help net interest margins for banks but compress bond portfolios and raise credit risk if energy-driven inflation slows the consumer. The sector is down ~5% YTD, and the Warsh transition adds policy uncertainty that keeps financials investors cautious heading into Q2.

08 — Term of the Day
FOMC Dissent

When one or more Federal Reserve policymakers formally vote against the majority's rate decision — signaling internal disagreement on the appropriate policy path. Dissents are rare and closely watched because they often preview future policy shifts.

10 — Powell Stays on as Fed Governor — Here's Why It Matters

Powell Stays on as Fed Governor — Here's Why It Matters

Jerome Powell announced he will remain on the Board of Governors after his chairmanship ends May 15, citing an ongoing renovations investigation into Fed headquarters. By staying, Powell retains a vote on the seven-member board, preventing the administration from filling his seat and potentially limiting how far a Warsh-led Fed can shift policy.
