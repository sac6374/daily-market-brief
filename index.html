Smart in 30 Seconds

Thursday, April 30 is the busiest macro day of the year so far. Q1 GDP came in at 2.0% annualized — up sharply from Q4 2025's 0.5% pace but still below the 2.3% consensus — while core PCE hit 3.2% year-over-year, accelerating from 3.0% and sitting well above the Fed's 2% target. The Fed held rates steady at 3.50%–3.75% yesterday in what was Jerome Powell's final FOMC press conference as Chair. Markets are now stuck between "growth is recovering" and "inflation won't cooperate."

01 — What Moved Markets Today

Earnings Night Split the Mag 7 in Two — and the Macro Data Didn't Help Either Side

Development 1

Alphabet surged more than 7% in premarket Thursday after a blockbuster Q1 report beat expectations across the board — a meaningful upside move for a stock with a roughly $2 trillion market cap, signaling that AI-driven ad revenue and cloud demand are both holding up. Amazon also climbed more than 2% after its own strong quarter, adding fuel to the Dow's 250-point, or 0.5%, gain on Thursday morning. These two were the bright spots in an otherwise uneven Big Tech night.

Development 2

Meta Platforms dropped 9% after hiking its full-year capital expenditure guidance to a range of $125 billion to $145 billion — that's up dramatically from prior forecasts — raising serious concerns about how long AI spending can outpace revenue returns. User growth also disappointed. Microsoft slipped 2% despite a solid quarter. The divergence matters: the market is now separating Mag 7 names that are monetizing AI from those that are still investing heavily into it, and it is pricing that gap aggressively.

Development 3

Caterpillar popped 5%, giving the Dow its biggest individual boost of the session, as the industrial giant beat quarterly estimates. Eli Lilly jumped 7% and raised its full-year sales outlook to between $82 billion and $85 billion, up from a prior range of $80 billion to $83 billion — a signal that GLP-1 drug demand remains a durable growth story. On the flip side, Brent crude rose to around $120 a barrel, up roughly 1.96%, as Trump rejected Iran's proposal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, keeping the U.S. naval blockade in place and energy prices elevated.

02 — Why It Mattered

Why investors cared.

This was the most data-dense 24 hours of 2026 so far: four Mag 7 earnings reports, a Fed decision, Q1 GDP, and PCE all landed within hours of each other. GDP bouncing from 0.5% to 2.0% is genuinely good news for growth, but core PCE accelerating to 3.2% — 120 basis points above the Fed's target — confirms the central bank has no easy path to cutting rates, especially now that Powell is stepping down as Chair on May 15.

The stagflation risk is real and getting louder. GDP is recovering but still below expectations, and inflation is re-accelerating — driven in large part by energy costs tied to the Iran conflict. The Fed is in no position to cut, but it can't hike either without risking a stall. Markets are pricing roughly one cut before year-end, and that number could easily move to zero if the next few data prints stay hot.

04 — What It Means

Breaking it down.

Rates & Treasury Yields

The Fed held its benchmark rate at 3.50%–3.75% at Wednesday's meeting — unchanged, as 99% of the market expected — but the 10-year Treasury yield jumped more than 5 basis points to 4.41% after the decision, moving up because a "hold with no cut signal" is a hawkish message when inflation is 3.2% and climbing. The 2-year Treasury also rose 9.3 basis points to 3.937%, its highest in two years, reflecting markets pricing out near-term rate relief. Higher yields matter because they raise borrowing costs for companies, governments, and consumers alike.

Stocks & Indexes

On Thursday morning, the Dow is up 250 points (+0.5%), the S&P 500 is up 0.4%, and the Nasdaq is up 0.6% — all recovering from Wednesday's flat-to-negative close, where the Dow fell 280 points (-0.57%) to 48,861 for a fifth straight losing session. The gains are being driven by Alphabet, Amazon, Caterpillar, and Eli Lilly. Importantly, those gains are being capped by Meta's 9% drop and Microsoft's 2% slide, keeping the S&P 500 and Nasdaq from a bigger bounce. The Russell 2000 small-cap index is outperforming major indexes year-to-date because small caps have less overseas exposure and are more insulated from geopolitical energy shocks.

Broader Economy

Q1 GDP came in at 2.0% annualized — a real improvement from the prior quarter's 0.5% pace, though it missed the 2.3% consensus. The acceleration was driven by government spending, exports, and investment, partially offset by slower consumer spending. Core PCE rose to 3.2% year-over-year, up from 3.0%, with gasoline and energy goods accounting for the largest chunk of consumer spending increases this month. Initial jobless claims fell sharply to 189,000 for the week ended April 25 — down 26,000 from 215,000 the prior week and well below consensus — signaling a still-resilient labor market.

What to Watch

Whether Apple's guidance and capex commentary aligns with or diverges from Meta's AI spending escalation — this sets the tone for tech going into May.

What to watch next

Apple Q1 earnings today (first report with CEO transition announced — Tim Cook stepping down Sept. 1, John Ternus taking over) · May 1 ISM Manufacturing PMI — first major activity survey post-GDP and post-FOMC · May 8 payrolls report — the next big labor market test before any Fed rate rethink

05 — Market Sector Lens

Who's moving and why.

Technology / AI — Diverging — winners vs. spenders

Alphabet's 7%+ surge and Amazon's 2%+ gain show the market rewarding AI names that are converting investment into revenue. Meta's 9% drop on its $125B–$145B capex guidance is the other side of that trade — the market is now asking "when does the spending stop and the returns start?" This split is the defining story in tech right now, and it will play out stock by stock through the rest of earnings season.

Energy — Hot — geopolitical premium intact

Brent crude is at approximately $120 a barrel — up roughly 2% Thursday and sharply higher over the past several weeks — because Trump rejected Iran's offer to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, signaling the U.S. naval blockade is holding until a nuclear deal is reached. That geopolitical premium in oil is the single biggest driver of headline PCE inflation right now, and it's also hurting global economies that depend on Middle Eastern supply. ConocoPhillips reports earnings today, making energy a sector to watch closely.

Industrials / Healthcare — Strong — both beating estimates

Caterpillar is up more than 160% over the past year and 41% year-to-date, and its latest beat reinforces that AI infrastructure buildout is driving real construction and equipment demand — a non-obvious beneficiary of the AI spending wave. Eli Lilly raised full-year sales guidance to $82B–$85B (up from $80B–$83B), with GLP-1 drugs continuing to post durable volume growth. Both names remind investors that the best AI trades aren't always AI stocks.

06 — Finance Career Lens

What people in finance are actually thinking about today.

For everyone in finance

This is what a "good news is complicated news" market feels like. GDP bounced back, earnings are mostly solid, and the labor market is holding. But oil is near $120, inflation is re-accelerating, the Fed is frozen, and the single biggest geopolitical wildcard — Iran — is nowhere near resolved. The market isn't crashing, but it isn't getting clear either. That uncertainty is the environment you're operating in.

Wealth Management

Energy inflation, frozen Fed, and client portfolio stress-testing Wealth managers are dealing with clients who are watching oil near $120, core PCE at 3.2%, and a Fed that cannot cut. The practical question on client calls right now is whether fixed income allocations are working as a hedge or a drag — with the 10-year at 4.41% (up from much lower earlier in the year), existing bond portfolios with lower-yielding holdings are underwater. This is the moment to understand bond duration risk, the difference between TIPS and nominal Treasuries as inflation hedges, and how energy-exposed vs. energy-insulated equities behave in a supply-shock inflation environment.

Investment Banking (Equity Capital Markets / M&A Advisory)

IPO market reality check — Pershing Square's scaled-back debut is a live case study Bill Ackman's Pershing Square IPO raised $5 billion, pricing at the low end of expectations after initially targeting $5B–$10B — and a far cry from the $25 billion floated two years ago. For ECM bankers, this is a textbook example of how market sentiment, geopolitical risk, and investor appetite compress deal size from mandate to close. Understanding why deals get downsized — and how to manage client expectations through that process — is a core skill in ECM.

Macro / Fixed Income Research

Today is the most consequential single morning for macro analysts in 2026 GDP at 2.0% (below consensus), core PCE at 3.2% (above prior), jobless claims at 189,000 (well below consensus), a leaderless Fed post-Powell, and oil near $120 — this is the exact data configuration that macro researchers and fixed income analysts build scenario frameworks around. The question they're answering right now: does the market price in zero cuts for 2026, or does slowing growth eventually force the Fed's hand even with sticky inflation? If you want to work in rates or macro strategy, this morning's data is your interview prep for the next three months.

08 — Term of the Day
Core PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures Price Index)

The Fed's preferred inflation gauge, measuring price changes in consumer spending while excluding volatile food and energy costs — currently running at 3.2% year-over-year, well above the Fed's 2% target, which is a key reason rate cuts remain off the table.
