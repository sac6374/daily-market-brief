Smart in 30 Seconds

Iran offered the U.S. a new proposal — reopen the Strait of Hormuz and end the war, but defer nuclear talks to a later stage. The White House confirmed receipt. Markets shrugged: stocks edged to fresh records while oil climbed anyway, because the nuclear program is a U.S. red line and traffic through Hormuz remains essentially zero. Meanwhile, Goldman Sachs raised its Brent oil forecast to $90/barrel for Q4 — nearly $30 above its pre-war estimate — citing record inventory draws. This week is one of the busiest in years: five Magnificent Seven companies report earnings, the Fed meets Wednesday, and the Bank of Japan and ECB also decide this week. The S&P 500 has now hit record highs five sessions in a row. The next catalyst is not diplomacy — it's earnings.

01 — What Moved Markets Today

Iran blinked — sort of. Markets shrugged — mostly. Earnings week begins.

Geopolitics — Iran's New Hormuz Proposal

Axios reported Monday morning, citing a U.S. official and two sources familiar with the matter, that Iran has offered to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and end the Gulf conflict, provided the U.S. agrees to defer nuclear negotiations to a later stage. White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt confirmed Trump and his national security team reviewed the proposal. The catch: Iran's nuclear program remains a firm U.S. red line, and Trump canceled the planned Islamabad trip for Vance, Witkoff, and Kushner on Saturday, citing "tremendous infighting and confusion" within Tehran's leadership. Iran's Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf said the Hormuz reopening is "impossible" as long as the U.S. blockade continues. Ship traffic through the strait remained near zero Monday, per Bloomberg. Oil rose despite the diplomatic signal — Brent crossed $100 to $108.23, WTI settled at $96.37 — because the proposal is seen as preliminary and the nuclear question is unresolved.

Goldman Sachs Revises Oil Forecast — Record Inventory Draws

In an April 27 note, Goldman Sachs raised its Q4 2026 Brent forecast to $90 per barrel from $80 previously — nearly $30 above its pre-war estimate. Analyst Daan Struyven cited "extreme" global inventory draws, estimating that 14.5 million barrels per day of Persian Gulf crude production losses are drawing global inventories at a record pace of 11–12 million barrels per day in April. Goldman noted that Gulf exports are not expected to normalize until end-June at the earliest, and warned that "even sharper demand losses could be required if the supply shock persists longer." Source: Bloomberg and CNBC, citing Goldman's April 27 note.

The Biggest Earnings Week of the Year — Five Magnificent Seven Names Report

Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon all report Wednesday after close. Apple reports Thursday. Together, these five companies represent roughly 25% of the S&P 500's market cap. As of Friday, 84% of S&P 500 companies that have reported beat earnings expectations — historically strong — and annual S&P 500 earnings growth is now tracking at 15.1%, per FactSet. The question this week is whether the Magnificent Seven's AI-driven growth narratives hold up under elevated oil costs, higher yields, and a forward P/E for the index approaching 21. Dan Ives of Wedbush said he expects "more good news on the horizon" — but the bar is high after each stock is up 10%+ this month alone.

02 — Why It Mattered

A deal offer that doesn't resolve the core issue. An oil forecast that explains why.

Iran's Hormuz proposal is being treated as a positive directional signal — the country is acknowledging the economic cost of the closure and looking for an exit. But deferring nuclear talks is not the same as offering a deal on nuclear weapons, and the Trump administration has consistently held that nuclear enrichment is non-negotiable. The market's reaction — stocks slightly higher, oil also higher — tells you what traders actually believe: the proposal is real but doesn't change the near-term supply picture. Hormuz stays closed until something concrete happens, and nothing concrete happened today.

Goldman's oil forecast revision is the more analytically important development. When the world's most prominent commodities research desk raises a quarterly price forecast by nearly $30 in a single note and describes inventory draws as "record" and "not sustainable," it's issuing a formal warning that the economic damage from this disruption is compounding — not easing. The revision also matters because it forces every analyst, portfolio manager, and corporate treasurer who follows Goldman to revisit their models. Inflation projections, earnings estimates for energy-intensive sectors, and Fed rate-cut timelines all move when Goldman moves oil.

The earnings week context is the critical overlay. The S&P 500 is at a record. Forward P/E is near 21. Rally breadth has narrowed — only 53% of S&P 500 stocks now trade above their 50-day moving average, down from 60% last week. What that means is the index is being carried by a smaller number of very large companies. If Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, or Amazon disappoint — on revenue, guidance, or AI capex commentary — the selloff would be concentrated and fast. There is no cushion in the price.

Microsoft announced Monday it is ending its exclusive partnership with OpenAI and terminating a revenue-sharing agreement. The stock dipped at the open. The read-through is significant for AI infrastructure investors: Microsoft's move signals it no longer needs OpenAI exclusivity to win in enterprise AI — it has its own models, Azure, and Copilot. That's actually a sign of strategic confidence, not weakness, but it creates uncertainty around OpenAI's valuation path.

04 — What It Means

Breaking it down.

Rates & Treasury Yields

10-year yield at 4.32%, up 1bp. The Fed meets Tuesday-Wednesday and is widely expected to hold rates unchanged — it's likely Powell's last meeting as chair. Markets now price only a 40% probability of any rate cut in 2026, down from 50% before Warsh's hearing last week. Goldman's oil revision makes rate cuts even harder to justify: sustained $90–100+ Brent means the inflation fight isn't over, regardless of how the nuclear talks end.

Stocks & Indexes

S&P 500 and Nasdaq both hit record closes Monday. But breadth is narrowing and the RSI for the S&P 500 is near 70 — technically overbought territory. The rally is now almost entirely dependent on the Magnificent Seven delivering earnings that justify current valuations. Domino's −10% on an earnings miss today is a reminder that elevated oil costs and soft consumer spending are real pressures outside of mega-cap tech.

Broader Economy

The Hormuz closure is starting to spread beyond oil. Invesco flagged disruptions extending to helium, aluminum, and sulfur. Domino's miss cited higher living costs and cautious consumer spending as drivers — the first clear consumer stress signal from a discretionary name this earnings season. Goldman's inventory draw estimate (11–12M bpd) is an extraordinary figure that, if sustained, implies supply shortfalls reaching consumers in the form of higher prices across many categories, not just gasoline.

What to Watch

Wednesday is again the fulcrum: four Magnificent Seven reports after close plus the Fed decision and Powell press conference. Any guidance cut or AI capex concern from Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, or Amazon would land the same day as the Fed's statement — compressing two major risk events into one session. Also watch for any White House response to Iran's Hormuz proposal, which the national security team is reviewing.

Goldman's warning in plain English

Goldman estimates global oil inventories are drawing down at 11–12 million barrels per day in April — a rate they describe as "not sustainable." That means either demand has to fall sharply (recession signal), or supply needs to be restored (Hormuz reopens), or prices rise further to ration demand. All three outcomes carry significant market implications. Their base case — Gulf exports normalize by end of June — would still leave inventories depleted and prices elevated through Q3.

Who's moving and why.

Semiconductors Historic Run

Chip stocks' RSI is above 80 — technically the most overbought part of the market. TSMC surged 6% in Asian trading Monday after a record quarter. Micron (+4%) and Sandisk (+7%) led U.S. gains. The AI infrastructure buildout is the dominant narrative. The risk: elevated RSI means a single disappointment in Alphabet or Microsoft's AI cloud commentary this week could trigger a fast pullback in the whole group.

Energy Goldman Upgrade

Brent at $108, WTI at $96, and Goldman raising Q4 forecasts to $90 Brent makes energy the clearest fundamental winner in the market right now. The sector is benefiting from both the physical supply shock and now an institutional endorsement that prices stay elevated through at least Q3. Chevron and Exxon report Friday — their capital allocation guidance on an extended $90+ oil environment will be closely watched.

Consumer Discretionary Stress Showing

Domino's Pizza fell 10% after reporting same-store sales growth of 0.9% vs. 2.7% expected and cutting its full-year outlook, citing higher living costs and cautious consumer spending. It's the clearest consumer stress signal yet this earnings season. Airlines, which were the biggest winners when oil briefly dropped to $82, are quietly struggling again with Brent back above $100.

Mega-cap Tech Earnings This Week

Apple, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet are all up 10%+ this month — a massive setup heading into earnings. Every one of those companies faces the same question: is AI capex generating enough revenue to justify the spend? Investors are also watching Microsoft's OpenAI split closely — the end of exclusivity is read as confidence, but any AI deal uncertainty could weigh on sentiment across the group before Wednesday's close.

The ability to hold all of that simultaneously — and know which variable matters most at each moment — is what separates someone who follows markets from someone who understands them. Wealth Management

Clients with index-heavy portfolios are more exposed to this week's earnings than they may realize. The WM framing is not "should we sell before earnings" — it's "do you understand what you actually own and why your portfolio moves the way it does?" Investment Banking

Two developments today are directly relevant to deal activity. First, Iran's Hormuz proposal — even if preliminary — is the first concrete signal that economic pressure is creating a path toward reopening. That changes the forward deal environment: if oil normalizes by summer, corporate confidence returns and M&A pipelines reopen. Second, Microsoft ending its OpenAI exclusivity is a corporate strategy event that will generate advisory and restructuring work. Any time a multi-billion-dollar partnership restructures, bankers get called. Know the read-through: it signals Microsoft is confident enough in its own AI capabilities that it no longer needs OpenAI's exclusivity as a competitive moat.

Options markets are pricing wide implied moves across Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon — each stock carrying a 6–9% implied swing. For S&T interviews: know what "implied move" means and how it's calculated from options premiums. 08 — Term of the Day
Market Breadth

A measure of how many stocks are participating in a market move, as opposed to just a handful of large ones. When market breadth is wide, most stocks are rising — which signals healthy, broad-based investor confidence. When breadth narrows, the index keeps climbing but only because a small number of very large companies are pulling it up, while most stocks are flat or falling. Today, only 53% of S&P 500 stocks trade above their 50-day moving average — down from 60% last week. The index is at a record. But if you look beneath the surface, the rally is increasingly being carried by the Magnificent Seven. Narrow breadth doesn't mean the market will fall — but it does mean a miss from one of those companies lands harder, because there's no broad base to absorb the shock.
