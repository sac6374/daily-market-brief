Smart in 30 Seconds

The Fed held rates again — no surprise — but three members dissented against language hinting at future cuts, pushing the 10-year yield to a one-month high of 4.43%. Meanwhile, four Mag 7 companies reported mostly strong Q1 results after Wednesday's close, lifting futures overnight, while oil hit a wartime high of $126 before pulling back as markets priced in possible new U.S. military action in Iran. April ends as one of the strongest months for equities in years, but the macro backdrop heading into May is genuinely complicated: hot oil, a hawkish Fed transition, and AI capex scrutiny all in the same week.

01 — What Moved Markets Today

Mag 7 Earnings, Powell's Exit, and $126 Oil: The Week That Defined April

Development 1

Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta all reported after Wednesday's close. Microsoft beat on earnings and revenue, with Azure cloud revenue up 40% year-over-year — a number that matters because cloud growth validates the AI capex thesis the whole market is pricing in. Amazon beat estimates and shares rose 4%. Alphabet beat on top and bottom lines, driven by Google Cloud strength. Meta was the outlier: it beat on earnings but hiked its full-year capex guidance to $125–$145 billion, up sharply, sending shares down ~9% as investors questioned whether the spend is getting ahead of the revenue.

Development 2

On Thursday morning, Eli Lilly surged ~7% after blowing past Q1 estimates. Mounjaro (diabetes) revenue was up 125% year-over-year to $8.66 billion, and Zepbound (obesity) was up 80% to $4.16 billion — together accounting for roughly two-thirds of Lilly's total sales of $19.7 billion. The company raised full-year sales guidance by $2 billion. This is a signal that GLP-1 drug demand remains structurally strong despite pricing pressure, which matters for anyone watching pharma sector valuations or healthcare M&A.

Development 3

Qualcomm surged 11% in early trading after topping Q2 earnings estimates. The real headline: a note in its release that "a leading hyperscaler custom silicon engagement is on track for initial shipments later this calendar year" — widely read as Qualcomm landing an AI chip deal with a major tech partner. Q3 guidance came in slightly below consensus due to memory supply constraints, but investors looked past it, lifting the broader semiconductor sector including Broadcom, Intel, and AMD.

Development 4

The Fed held its benchmark rate unchanged on Wednesday — widely expected — but the vote wasn't clean. Three members dissented, objecting to language they viewed as an "easing bias," making this the most hawkish read of a hold decision in recent memory. Traders are now pricing roughly a one-in-three chance of a rate hike by April 2027. Chair Powell confirmed he will stay on the Board of Governors after his chairmanship ends May 15, while nominee Kevin Warsh moves toward Senate confirmation. The leadership transition adds a layer of policy uncertainty heading into the second half of 2026.

02 — Why It Mattered

Why investors cared.

April's 9% S&P 500 rally was built on two pillars — earnings proving AI spending is producing real revenue, and oil stabilizing enough that inflation expectations didn't spiral. Both are now wobbling: Meta's capex raise raised ROI questions, oil briefly hit a four-year high of $126/barrel on Iran escalation fears, and 10-year yields jumped on the hawkish Fed dissent. The market is not in crisis, but the easy tailwinds of early April have faded.

The core tension right now: Big Tech earnings justify high valuations only if AI capex translates into revenue growth, but Meta just told the market it's spending even more before it monetizes what it already has. At the same time, oil above $107 keeps inflation elevated and gives the new Fed leadership reason to stay hawkish — meaning rate cuts that investors expected this year may not come. Something has to give: either AI revenue scales fast enough to satisfy investors, or the market reprices risk materially higher.

04 — What It Means

Breaking it down.

Rates & Treasury Yields

The 10-year Treasury yield climbed 7 basis points to 4.43% on Thursday — its highest level in about a month — after the Fed's hold came with three dissenters pushing back on any hint of future cuts. The 2-year yield crossed above 3.95%, meaning short-term rates, which are more sensitive to Fed policy, moved even more sharply. Higher yields raise the discount rate applied to future earnings, which is why growth and tech stocks are most exposed when the yield moves up — this is the math behind why rate decisions matter so much to equity valuations.

Stocks & Indexes

The S&P 500 is up roughly 9% for April — on pace for its best month since November 2020 — and the Nasdaq is heading for a 14% April gain, its best since April 2020. Thursday morning's action showed indexes holding fractional gains as Mag 7 earnings broadly satisfied the bar, but Meta's 9% drop and oil's overnight spike to $126 kept a lid on enthusiasm. Apple reports after Thursday's close — the last Mag 7 name of the season — and its result will set the tone for May.

Broader Economy

Thursday's PCE inflation data showed prices holding above the Fed's 2% target in March, confirming the Fed's caution is warranted. Initial jobless claims for the week ended April 25 came in at 189,000 — down from 215,000 the prior week, a sign the labor market remains resilient. GDP growth for Q1 came in slightly below expectations. The combination of sticky inflation, solid employment, and below-trend growth is the definition of a difficult macro environment for a central bank to navigate — especially with an oil shock layered on top.

05 — Market Sector Lens

Who's moving and why.

Technology — Mixed but resilient

Alphabet, Amazon, and Microsoft all delivered, with Azure's 40% cloud growth and Amazon's beat leading the way. The sector's problem is Meta: its $125–$145 billion capex range for 2026 is up sharply and investors want to see revenue justify it. Qualcomm's AI chip win and the broader semis rally — Broadcom, Intel, AMD all higher — show that the infrastructure build-out thesis is still intact at the component level even as platform-level spend faces scrutiny.

Energy — Extreme volatility

Brent hit $126 intraday Thursday — its highest since 2022 — before pulling back to ~$115 after reports that the U.S. military is briefing Trump on potential new Iran action. WTI is at $107, up ~84% year-over-year, as the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed and U.S. inventories show steep drawdowns. Energy companies like Chevron and Exxon, reporting Friday, are printing record cash flows at these prices, but any diplomatic breakthrough could erase gains fast.

Healthcare / Pharma — Strong outperformer

Eli Lilly's blowout quarter — $19.7 billion in revenue, up 56% year-over-year — is the story of the sector. GLP-1 drugs Mounjaro and Zepbound together drove roughly two-thirds of total sales, and the FDA approved Foundayo, Lilly's oral GLP-1 pill, adding a new growth vector. Merck also beat, with Keytruda (cancer) bolstering results. Healthcare is increasingly viewed as a defensive-growth hybrid: real earnings growth that doesn't depend on AI capex ROI.

06 — Finance Career Lens

What people in finance are actually thinking about today.

For everyone in finance

Gas prices at the pump are averaging close to $4.30/gallon nationally in April — the highest monthly average since 2022 — because WTI crude above $107 flows directly into refinery costs and then to the gas station. Diesel is even worse, averaging over $5.80/gallon, which raises costs for trucking, logistics, and anything that ships. If you've noticed grocery prices creeping higher, energy costs embedded throughout the supply chain are a big reason why.

Wealth Management

Client Portfolio Rebalancing: When Oil, Rates, and Tech All Move Together Wealth managers are dealing with a rare scenario where three major asset classes are all repricing simultaneously: oil up nearly 100% year-over-year (inflation risk), 10-year yields climbing (bond price pressure), and Mag 7 stocks up 14% in one month (concentration risk). For client conversations, the core question is rebalancing: portfolios that weren't trimmed on tech's April rally may now be overweight growth heading into a potentially hawkish Fed transition. Energy stocks and healthcare are acting as natural hedges — Eli Lilly up sharply, General Dynamics up 5% on defense orders, defensive names holding ground. Advisors who can explain the yield–equity relationship and why sector rotation matters in this environment will stand out in client reviews and in job interviews.

Investment Banking (Energy & Natural Resources)

Oil above $107 means record deal flow in energy — this desk is active When oil sustains triple-digit pricing, energy companies have cash to deploy — into acquisitions, refinancing, and capital raises. The UAE's shock exit from OPEC, ongoing supply disruption through the Strait of Hormuz, and record U.S. crude exports are all structural story lines that energy IB teams are pitching. If you're interviewing in E&R coverage or commodities-adjacent roles, walk in knowing the Brent–WTI spread ($8–$12/barrel currently), what's driving it (Middle East shipping costs), and why U.S. producers are the relative beneficiaries.

Equity Research (Technology)

AI capex ROI is the research question of 2026 — analysts are being tested on it daily Every Mag 7 earnings call this week came down to one question: is the AI spending producing enough revenue to justify the cost? Analysts who modeled Azure's 40% cloud growth against Microsoft's capex curve, or who flagged Meta's $145 billion capex ceiling as a margin risk before the print, were ahead of consensus. If you want to break into tech equity research, being able to build a simple capex-to-revenue bridge model for a hyperscaler is now table stakes. The Mag 7 collectively are expected to spend $650 billion in capex in 2026 — knowing which companies are generating the highest return on that spend is what separates good research from generic coverage.

Macro / Fixed Income Strategy

The Fed transition and rate path uncertainty make this the most interesting desk in the market right now Jerome Powell's tenure ends May 15. Kevin Warsh, his likely successor, is seen as more hawkish. Three Fed members just dissented on a hold — an unusually strong signal. Traders are pricing a one-in-three chance of a rate hike by April 2027. If you're targeting macro strategy, rates trading, or fixed income research roles, the current setup is a gift: the yield curve, Fed composition, inflation dynamics (oil-driven PCE), and central bank credibility are all live debates. Being able to articulate why the 10-year yield jumped 7 bps on a rate hold — and what that means for duration positioning — is exactly what interviewers are probing for.

08 — Term of the Day
Dissent (FOMC)

When one or more Federal Reserve policymakers vote against the majority decision at a meeting — a rare signal that carries outsized weight because it signals the policy consensus is fracturing, which markets interpret as a potential shift in direction.
